# Bristlenose — Manifesto

_10 Mar 2026_

## The problem

The analytics-first product manager has won. "Data-driven" means dashboards, funnels, and A/B tests. It answers *what* users do but never *why*. The qualitative work that explains the numbers — talking to people, hearing their words, understanding their context — has been squeezed out.

Not because people don't value it. Because the tooling assumes you're either a full-time researcher with enterprise budget or you're not doing research at all.

**The professional UXR tool market has bifurcated:**

On one side: enterprise SaaS platforms (Dovetail, UserTesting, Medallia, Qualtrics) that take months to get through procurement, cost per-seat prices that assume dedicated research teams, try to own all your user data, and bundle every CX/NPS/survey feature imaginable. Even a 10-person research team at a bank spends months getting approval.

On the other side: nothing. The PM does three interviews, takes notes in Google Docs, maybe drops quotes into a Miro board, and that's "the research." Not because they're lazy or don't care — because the gap between free-form notes and proper analysis tooling is a cliff, not a ramp.

## What got lost

There was a time when stickies and Sharpies were serious methodology — and not just accessible, but cognitively superior to what replaced it.

You read the transcript. You pick a quote. You handwrite it onto a sticky note. The physical constraint of the sticky forces editorial judgement — you can't paste the whole paragraph, so you have to decide what matters. That act of compression is itself analysis. And while you're writing, you're thinking about what the participant said and what they meant. By the time the pen leaves the paper, you've already done the interpretive work.

Then you look at a wall of 1,000 stickies and your brain knows where to put it — because your motor system, your spatial reasoning, and your semantic understanding were all engaged simultaneously while you wrote. That's not a metaphor. That's embodied cognition. The friction was the feature.

Digital tools lost all of this. Highlight a quote in Dovetail, drag it to a tag. You've moved text from one container to another. You haven't *thought* about it. The grunt work disappeared, but so did the thinking that was hidden inside the grunt work.

Stickies and Sharpies were a better methodology in every way — except that the result wasn't a shareable digital artefact. It didn't persist, didn't travel, and couldn't be "collaborated" on. So it died.

## Who this is for

Most research teams — even in 1,000-person companies — are small. Three people, maybe five. Most small digital outfits have no UXR function at all, just PMs and designers doing ad-hoc interviews when they can make time. Freelance researchers working with startups. Agency teams who need to turn around findings in days, not weeks.

These people don't need a platform. They need a tool.

## What Bristlenose believes

**Affordable tools for user research, back in the hands of everyone.**

1. **Research is a method, not a department.** Anyone who talks to users and listens carefully is doing research. The tooling shouldn't gatekeep that behind enterprise procurement.

2. **The quote is the atomic unit.** A participant's own words, in context, are the strongest evidence a product team can hold. Everything else — themes, sentiments, frameworks — is interpretation layered on top. The quotes come first.

3. **Analysis should be fast enough to be habitual.** If it takes two weeks to produce findings, teams will skip it. If it takes an afternoon, they'll do it every sprint.

4. **Your data is yours.** Not because cloud is bad — but because research data contains real people's words, names, and experiences. Researchers should choose where that lives, not have the choice made for them by a platform's terms of service.

5. **Good defaults, no lock-in.** A folder of recordings in, a browsable report out. No account, no onboarding flow, no thirty-minute setup. Bring your own LLM provider — or don't, and use a local model.

6. **The tool should vanish.** The best research tool is one you forget you're using. Bristlenose should feel like a well-organised desk, not a product with opinions.

## What this is not

Bristlenose is not a replacement for skilled researchers. It doesn't do the thinking. It transcribes, extracts, clusters, and presents — but a human decides what matters, what to name the themes, which quotes to lead with, and what to recommend. The judgement is yours. The tool just makes the grunt work disappear so you can get to the judgement faster.

It's also not an enterprise platform. No team management, no permissions, no audit logs, no SSO. If you need those, the enterprise tools exist and they're fine. Bristlenose is for the teams those tools forgot about.
